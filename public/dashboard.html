Employment

Many international students studying in the United States would like to work in order to earn some extra money or to help with the bills. However, as an international student, you are very limited in the work you are allowed to participate in. All types of employment must be approved by your DSO prior to you working.

Working in the U.S. without the proper authorization is a serious violation of the terms of your F-1 visa. If you work begin working without approval, your DSO is required by federal law to report the violation through SEVIS and your I-20 may be terminated. This means you will have to leave the U.S. immediately and may not be allowed to return.

That being said, there are certain circumstances that allow you to work while studying in the U.S. These are listed below but if you have any questions regarding your employment options, speak to your DSO.

On Campus Employment

If you are in status (your SEVIS record is active), then you are eligible for on-campus employment. This allows to you work at your school or at a location off-campus that is affiliated with your school. An example of this would be working at your school’s gym, bookstore, cafeteria, library… the list goes on. The opportunities will vary by each school.

Speak to your DSO to see what opportunities are available to you.

Optional Practical Training (OPT)

Optional Practical Training or OPT is an important employment benefit offered to international students. With OPT authorization, you are legally able to work anywhere in the United States for up to 12-months as long as the work relates to your field of study. In order to be approved for OPT, you must submit an application to USCIS requesting permission. This process usually takes 90-days but when you are approved, you will receive an Employment Authorization Document (or EAD Card). There are two different types of OPT that you may be able to apply for:

Pre-Completion OPT and Post-Completion OPT

Pre-completion OPT

If you are approved for pre-completion OPT, you are able to work while you are enrolled in your program.

During your academic term, you are able to work up to 20-hours per week. During a break between terms or on a vacation quarter/semester, you can work full-time.

Any amount of time you work with pre-completion OPT will be deducted from your post-completion OPT time.

Example: If you apply and are approved for part-time pre-completion OPT from 09/01/2018 to 01/01/2019, then you would now have 10-months of post-completion remaining.

4 months of part-time OPT = 2 months of full-time OPT

12 months - 2 months = 10vmonths of post-completion OPT remaining

Post-completion OPT

If you are approved for post-completion OPT, you are able to work in the U.S. for up to 12-months as long as your job relates to your field of study and you work full-time (at least 20-hours per week).

Post-completion OPT is a great opportunity for you to gain work experience before you return home country or before you begin a new academic program.

Example: You successfully complete a 2-year associate’s degree at a community college in computer science, work for a year as a web developer before you begin your bachelor’s degree program at a university in software engineering.

STEM Extension

Students who earn a bachelor’s, master’s, or doctoral degree in a STEM major (science, technology, engineering, or math) are eligible for a 24-month extension in addition to the 12-month post-completion OPT period. This means the qualifying student can work for up to three years in the U.S.!

Reduced Course Load

In order to maintain your immigration status, you are required you to be enrolled full-time each quarter/semester. For most undergraduate programs, this means 12-credits.

However, there are circumstances which will allow you to under-enroll and still keep your status. These circumastances are known as a Reduction in Course Load or an RCL

Medical RCL

If you are experiencing a medical condition that prevents you from attending all or some of your classes, you can receive authorization to take less than the required amount of credits. A medical condition can be many different things such as illness, depression, anxiety, injury, pregnancy, etc.

In order to be approved, you will need to provide the following documents to your International Advisor:

  • Your school’s Reduced Course Load Form or Medical Leave Form
    • Different schools may require different forms. Check with your International Advisor to ask which specific from your school requires
  • A signature from a doctor authorizing either a full or partial reduction in course load

*This can only be authorized by a U.S. licensed medical doctor, doctor of osteopathy, or licensed clinical psychologist. Other healthcare professionals such as a nurse, nurse practitioner, or physical therapist cannot authorize a reduced course load

Graduating Quarter/Semester

If you are in your final quarter or semester of study, you only need to the classes that need in order to graduate. For example, if you only need to one classes in order to complete your bachelor’s degree, than you would qualify for a reduced course load.

In order to be approved, you will need to provide the following documents to your International Advisor:

  • A reduced course load form or graduating quarter/semester form

*Different schools may require different forms. Check with your International Advisor to ask which specific from your school requires

Concurrent Enrollment

If your current school does not offer a certain course you want or need, you may be able to take this course at a different school with Concurrent Enrollment. The rules of Concurrent Enrollment state that as long as you are enrolled for a certain number of credits at the school where your I-20 was issued (your primary school), you can take a class at another school (a secondary school) to equal full-time enrollment.

Example: Enroll in at least 7-credits at your primary school and 5-credits at your second school to equal 12-credits.

In order to learn more about Concurrent Enrollment at your school, speak to your International Advisor to see if this is a possibility for you. Each school will have a different policy regarding this but you generally will need the following documents to be approved:

  • A reduced course load form or concurrent enrollment form
  • A copy of your schedule from second school showing the course you are taking
Initial Academic Difficulty

If you are in your first quarter or semester of college and are having trouble with your classes, you may be eligible for a reduction in course load if you meet one of the following conditions:

  • Improper course placement
  • Initial difficulty with reading requirements
  • Initial difficulty with the English language
  • Unfamiliarity with US teaching methods

The Rules:

  • This authorization can only be used in the first academic term
  • You must maintain half of the course load normally required
    • Example: You must be enrolled in 6-credits if you normally are required to be enrolled for 12-credits
  • You must enroll in a full course of study in the following quarter/semester

In order to learn more about Initial Academic Difficulty at your school, speak to your International Advisor to see if this is a possibility for you

Transfers

Whether you have completed your associate's degree at a community college and are ready to switch to a 4-year university or simply want to change schools, transfers are an important step for international students.

A “transfer” in this context means moving your I-20 (or SEVIS record) from your current school to your new school. This is generally a simple process but it can be dfficult to understand if you have never transferred your I-20 before.

Transfer-In School Process
  • Identify the school or schools that you want to attend and apply
    • Be sure to submit your application to the new school by the application deadline
  • Your new school will give you a form called a Transfer-in form or a Status Verification form or something similar
    • This form lets your new school know that you are attending a school in the US and that you have an active I-20
  • In most cases, you will fill out a portion of the form and then submit it to your International Advisor at your current school
  • Your advisor will complete the rest of the form and send it to your new school
Transfer-Out School Process

In order for your current school to release (or transfer) your I-20 to your new school, you will need to submit two documents to your advisor:

  • Your current school's Transfer-out form
  • The acceptance letter from your new school

The Rules

Once your record is transferred, you must begin at your new school on the next available start date or within five-months; whichever comes first.
